MedRed+

One app where any Indian family — even grandma — can understand reports, follow prescriptions, store health records for life, and find the right doctor.

The Vision

A health revolution for non-technical India

Most Indian families can't read their own medical reports, forget prescription timings, carry physical files of records, and don't know which doctor to trust. MedRed fixes all four — in Hindi and English, designed for the least technical person in the family.

Read this before we build anything

Risks & solutions

Each risk is shown like an abnormal lab value: the problem, its severity, and the prescribed fix.

R-01 · Pathy Recommender = legal & ethical landmine

RISK: HIGH

In India, only Registered Medical Practitioners can give treatment advice (Telemedicine Practice Guidelines 2020, NMC rules). If our AI says "use homeopathy for this hormonal problem" and someone delays real treatment for PCOS or thyroid disease, the harm — and the liability — lands on us. Important: a "we are not responsible" disclaimer helps, but on its own it does NOT fully protect us if a court finds we gave medical advice that caused harm.

Prescribed fix — all three together
  • Reframe as routing, not treatment: "For this type of problem, people usually consult a [dermatologist / homeopath / ayurvedic doctor]" — we recommend a specialist type, never a treatment.
  • Disclaimer + forced acknowledgement: your notification idea, made mandatory — user must tap "I understand this is not medical advice, I will consult a doctor" before seeing the suggestion.
  • Emergency red-flag blocker: chest pain, blood in stool, infant fever, breathing trouble → no recommendation at all, only "see a doctor now" + nearby doctor list.

R-02 · "Teachers as doctors" — registration is mandatory

RISK: HIGH

Only registered practitioners can consult: NMC register for allopathy, CCH for homeopathy, CCIM for ayurveda. Students cannot consult. Cross-pathy prescribing (a homeopath prescribing allopathic medicine) is illegal per Supreme Court rulings.

Prescribed fix
  • Verify every listed doctor's registration number against the official register before they go live — make the verified badge visible to users (it's also a trust feature).
  • Riya & Aman's teachers are fine if and only if they hold current registration — collect registration numbers during onboarding.
  • Each doctor consults only within their own pathy on our platform.

R-03 · Health data law — DPDP Act 2023

RISK: HIGH

We will hold the most sensitive category of personal data. DPDP requires explicit consent, purpose limitation, breach notification, and consent per individual — the family feature means each adult member consents for themselves, not the subscriber for everyone.

Prescribed fix
  • Consent screen per family member at first login (adults consent for themselves; parents for minors).
  • Store data in India (Supabase region / hosting choice matters), encrypt reports at rest.
  • Build a "download / delete my data" button from day one — retrofitting is painful.

R-04 · "Gemini is free" won't survive real users

RISK: MEDIUM

Free-tier rate limits will choke at even ~100 daily users doing OCR + explanations. If our architecture assumes one provider, a pricing change forces a rewrite.

Prescribed fix
  • Build one internal AI interface (e.g., explainReport(), parsePrescription()) — Gemini today, swap to Claude/GPT behind it without touching the app.
  • Cache explanations: same test + similar values = reuse, don't re-call the API.

R-05 · Handwriting OCR errors on prescriptions

RISK: MEDIUM

A misread dose is dangerous — "0.5mg" read as "5mg" is a real failure mode, not a UX bug.

Prescribed fix
  • Your confirm-before-save flow, made stronger: show extracted medicine names against a real drug database (match suggestions), and highlight low-confidence fields in red for the user to check.
  • Never auto-schedule reminders from unconfirmed scans.

R-06 · Doctor marketplace cold-start

RISK: MEDIUM

Marketplaces need both sides at once: no doctors → no patients → no doctors. Launching consultations on day one splits our tiny team's energy.

Prescribed fix
  • Ship the tools (reader, locker, reminders) first — they work with zero doctors and build a user base.
  • Launch consultations only after we have users to send the first 15 doctors — then the ₹500–2000 sessions have real demand behind them.
Research answer · Government health stack

ABHA / ABDM — yes, we can integrate, and the API is free

India's Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission (ABDM) already gives every citizen a 14-digit health ID (ABHA) and a national health-record exchange. Instead of competing with it, MedRed builds on top of it as the friendly family layer.

Is it chargeable?No — the APIs are free. ABDM is a government mission; there are no API fees. The real cost is developer time (full integrations typically take 3–6 months, but our v1 scope is much smaller — see below).
How to get accessRegister on the ABDM Sandbox (sandbox.abdm.gov.in) → receive a client bridge ID + secret → build and test against sandbox APIs → pass certification → production access.
MilestonesM1 — ABHA number creation & verification (login with health ID). M2 — become a Health Information Provider (publish records users upload). M3 — Health Information User (fetch a user's records from hospitals/labs, with consent).
What v1 needsOnly M1 + Health Locker APIs: let users create/link their ABHA and store records against it. M2/M3 come later when we integrate with labs (Phase-3 partnerships fit perfectly here).
Technical formatHealth records exchange in FHIR R4 (a standard JSON format). We'll need a small transformation layer; NHA provides SDKs, and third-party middleware exists if we want to skip the plumbing.
Consent modelABDM has a built-in Consent Manager — patients approve/revoke every data share with OTP. This actually solves part of our DPDP work for us.
Why this is a marketing weapon, not just tech: "MedRed is linked with the Government of India's ABHA health ID" is the trust line that convinces a skeptical parent. Frame it in the app as "Link your national health ID" — many users already have an ABHA from government hospitals and Ayushman Bharat.
One caution: certification takes time and FHIR is fiddly. Recommendation: build our own locker (Supabase storage) for launch, add ABHA linking in v1.5 — don't let government certification block the first release.
